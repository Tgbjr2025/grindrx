# Stylish Pantry Cabinet — Build Plan

**For:** Mom
**Finished size:** 72" H × 60" W × 18" D (6' tall × 5' wide × 18" deep)
**Style:** Shaker — clean, flat-panel, timeless. Works equally well stained or painted.
**Layout:** Top half = two adjustable-shelf compartments behind doors. Bottom half = doors *and* a bank of drawers.

---

## 1. Design at a glance

```
        60" wide
 ┌───────────────────────┐  ─┐
 │  DOOR   │ │   DOOR     │   │  Upper section (~36" tall)
 │  shelves│ │  shelves   │   │  adjustable shelves behind
 │  behind │ │  behind    │   │  a pair of tall doors
 │         │ │            │   │
 ├─────────┴─┴────────────┤  ─┤  Mid rail / counter line
 │ ▭▭▭▭▭▭▭ │ │  DOOR      │   │  Lower section (~34" tall)
 │ ▭▭▭▭▭▭▭ │ │  (pots/    │   │  LEFT bay  = 3 drawers
 │ ▭▭▭▭▭▭▭ │ │   bulk)    │   │  RIGHT bay = 1 door over
 │ ▭▭▭▭▭▭▭ │ │            │   │              1 deep drawer
 ├─────────┴─┴────────────┤  ─┤
 │      toe kick 4"       │   │  Recessed kick
 └───────────────────────┘  ─┘
```

Because 60" is too wide for a single span (shelves sag, doors get unwieldy), the
cabinet is built around a **center partition** that splits it into two ~29" bays.
This makes shelves stiff, doors a sensible size, and the whole thing far stronger.

You can mix the lower layout however your mom cooks — the plan below uses
**3 drawers on the left, door + 1 deep drawer on the right.** Swap freely.

---

## 2. Materials & wood choice

**Recommended combo (the "looks great" sweet spot):**

| Part | Material | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Carcass (box, partition, shelves) | 3/4" veneer-core plywood (birch, maple, or white oak) | Stable, flat, strong, no sag with the center divider |
| Back panel | 1/2" plywood | Stiffens the box, keeps it square |
| Face frame | 3/4" solid hardwood, milled to ~1-1/2" wide | The "furniture" look; hides ply edges |
| Doors & drawer fronts | 3/4" solid hardwood rails/stiles + 1/4" flat panel | Classic Shaker |
| Drawer boxes | 1/2" Baltic birch ply (sides), 1/4" ply bottoms | Durable, smooth |
| Toe kick & trim | Solid hardwood | Matches face frame |

**Species options:**
- **White oak** — modern, beautiful grain, takes a natural/clear finish gorgeously. Premium look.
- **Maple or birch** — smooth, light; great if painting OR for a light natural finish.
- **Cherry** — warms to a rich reddish-brown over time; very "heirloom."
- **Poplar** — cheapest, paint-grade only (knots/green streaks hide under paint).

> **Stain vs. paint:** If your mom wants to *show the wood*, go oak/cherry/maple
> with a clear or light finish. If she wants a *color* (classic creamy white,
> sage green, navy), build doors/face frame from **poplar** + maple ply and paint —
> it's cheaper and looks crisp.

## 4. Tools

**Essential:** table saw (or track saw + straightedge), drill/driver, pocket-hole
jig (Kreg), clamps (several long ones), tape measure, square, sander, brad nailer.

**Very helpful:** miter saw, router (for door panels / edge profiles), shelf-pin
jig, level, stud finder (for wall anchoring), card scraper.

**Hardware:**
- Concealed Euro hinges (full-overlay) **or** Shaker butt hinges — 2 per door
- Full-extension drawer slides (soft-close, 18") — 1 pair per drawer
- Adjustable shelf pins (5mm) + drilled holes
- Knobs/pulls (let Mom pick — this is where personality shows)
- 2-1/2" cabinet screws + washers for wall anchoring
- Wood glue, pocket screws, 1-1/4" brads, finish of choice

---

## 5. Build sequence — the carcass

1. **Cut all plywood parts** to the sizes in §3. Label every piece. Keep factory
   edges for reference and cut oversize parts slightly long, then trim to final.
2. **Drill shelf-pin holes** in the sides and both faces of the center partition
   *before assembly* (much easier flat on a bench). Use a shelf-pin jig; two
   columns per bay, in the upper section only.
3. **Join bottom + sides + partition.** Glue and pocket-screw (or use dados for a
   stronger joint) the bottom deck between the sides, with the center partition
   centered. Build it sitting on the **toe-kick** assembly (a 4"-tall ladder frame
   set back 3" from the front).
4. **Add the top**, then the **fixed mid shelf** at ~38" from the floor (this is
   your "counter line" dividing upper/lower). Check for square with diagonals —
   they must match.
5. **Rabbet the back edges** and install the **1/2" back**, glued and brad-nailed.
   The back is what locks the cabinet square — don't skip it.

> **Weight tip:** A 6×5 cabinet is heavy. Consider building it as **two stacked
> units** (an upper carcass + a lower carcass that bolt together) so you can carry
> and install them. Same look, far easier to move into Mom's house. Highly
> recommended if there are stairs.

---

## 6. Drawers (lower-left bay + lower-right deep drawer)

1. Measure the finished drawer opening. **Subtract 1"** total width for the slides
   (1/2" each side). Box height ≈ opening minus ~1".
2. Build boxes from 1/2" Baltic birch: glue + brad, or for nicer boxes cut
   **drawer-lock or rabbet joints**. Capture a **1/4" plywood bottom** in a groove.
3. Mount **full-extension soft-close slides**. Get the box gliding smoothly *before*
   attaching fronts.
4. **Drawer fronts** (solid Shaker panels) go on last as false fronts, screwed from
   inside the box so you can adjust the reveal evenly. Use spacers/tape to set
   consistent gaps.

Suggested left-bay drawers: top ~6", middle ~8", bottom ~10" (deeper for pots).

---

## 7. Doors (Shaker, flat-panel)

For each door:
- **2 stiles + 2 rails** of 3/4" hardwood, ~2-1/4" wide.
- Cut a **1/4" groove** centered on the inner edges.
- Join with cope-and-stick router bits, or simpler **stub tenons** (a tongue on the
  rails into the stile grooves) glued + clamped.
- Float a **1/4" flat panel** in the grooves — do **not** glue the panel; let it
  move seasonally.
- Upper doors are tall (~36"); lower door ~30". Size to your openings with
  appropriate overlay for your hinges (3/8" or 1/2" typical full-overlay).

Hang doors, adjust hinges so all gaps (reveals) are even and consistent. This even
1/8" reveal everywhere is what makes the piece read as "professional."

---

## 8. Finishing (this is where it earns "looks great")

1. **Sand** progressively to 180 grit (220 if staining). Ease all sharp edges
   slightly so they feel soft and won't chip.
2. **Pre-stain conditioner** if using maple/cherry/birch + stain (prevents
   blotching). Skip for oak.
3. **Stain / dye** to taste, wipe off, let dry fully.
   - Natural look: skip stain, go straight to clear.
4. **Topcoat:** 2–3 coats of a durable clear — **water-based polyurethane**
   (stays clear, low odor) or **wipe-on poly** (foolproof, hand-rubbed look).
   Sand lightly with 320 between coats.
   - If **painting:** prime (shellac-based BIN or quality bonding primer), sand,
     then 2 coats of cabinet enamel. Spray if you can; otherwise a fine foam roller
     + brush for edges.
5. Finish **doors/drawer fronts off the cabinet**, both sides, for an even result.

---

## 9. Installation

1. Locate wall studs; mark their centers.
2. With a helper, set the cabinet (or stacked units) in place, **shim level** front-
   to-back and side-to-side. A tall cabinet *must* be plumb or doors swing open/shut
   on their own.
3. **Anchor to studs** through the back rail with 2-1/2"+ screws and washers — at
   least 4 points. A 6-foot cabinet is a tip-over risk; anchoring is mandatory,
   especially around kids.
4. Add **scribe molding** or caulk at the wall seam if the wall is uneven, and a
   small **crown or cove** at the top if you want a more built-in, furniture look.
5. Install knobs/pulls, set shelves, do final hinge/drawer adjustments.

---

## 10. Rough cost & time

- **Materials:** ~$350–$700 depending on species (oak/cherry high end, poplar+ply
  paint-grade low end) + ~$150–$250 in hardware (soft-close slides and good hinges
  add up).
- **Time:** A weekend builder should plan **3–5 working days**: 1 day cutting/carcass,
  1 day drawers, 1 day doors, 1–2 days finishing (with dry time).
